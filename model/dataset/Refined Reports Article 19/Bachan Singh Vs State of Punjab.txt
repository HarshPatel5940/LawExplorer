Headnotes:
- The case of Bachan Singh vs State of Punjab was heard by the Supreme Court of India on August 16, 1982.
- The case dealt with the constitutionality of the death penalty in India and the sentencing procedure for capital offenses.
- The court upheld the constitutionality of section 302 of the Indian Penal Code, which prescribes death or life imprisonment as punishment for murder.
- The court also found that section 354(3) of the Code of Criminal Procedure, which provides for the sentencing procedure for capital offenses, was constitutional.
- The court held that the right to life is not one of the rights mentioned in Article 19(1) of the Constitution, and the six fundamental freedoms guaranteed under Article 19(1) are not absolute rights.
- The court distinguished between "public order" and "law and order" and stated that not all murders disturb or affect public order.
- The court discussed the powers of the Supreme Court to lay down standards or norms restricting the imposition of the death penalty to a narrow category of murders.
- The court held that the provision of the death penalty for murder in section 302 of the Penal Code is not unreasonable and is in the public interest.
- The court discussed the interpretation of Articles 19 and 21 of the Constitution and the acceptance of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights.
- The court held that death penalty for murder does not violate the basic structure of the Constitution.
- Overall, the court upheld the constitutionality of the death penalty in India and the sentencing procedure for capital offenses.

Case History: The case was brought before the Supreme Court of India after Bachan Singh was convicted of murder and sentenced to death by the trial court. Bachan Singh challenged the constitutionality of the death penalty and the sentencing procedure for capital offenses.

2. Whether the sentencing procedure for capital offenses is constitutional.
3. Whether the death penalty serves any penological purpose.

- Section 354(3) of the Code of Criminal Procedure: Provides for the sentencing procedure for capital offenses.
- Article 19(1) of the Constitution: Guarantees six fundamental freedoms, which are not absolute rights.
- Article 21 of the Constitution: Guarantees the right to life.

Holding(s):
